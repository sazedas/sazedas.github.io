Understanding Potential Users, and Other Stakeholders

Overview

Freerice is a non-profit ad-supported system that allows users to play a quiz-like game where correct answers result in the donation of rice through the World Food Programme. The website extols the vocabulary game as a tool to expand their mental lexicon in ways such as being able to better formulate ideas, write more effective papers, speak more precisely, and read faster. Through this website, users can use their knowledge to help end world hunger. Despite this noble cause, the user interface is clunky and unappealing, and the main activity that allows people to donate rice is boring. The web siteâ€™s poor execution prevents it from being a product that most users would want to use.


The study objectives include learning how users interact with freerice. It was known which age population(s) most frequently used freerice and whether the purpose of it was for donating or learning. Finding patterns were anticipated regarding the aesthetics of the website. The interviews were guided to focus more on the usability in terms of how long the users spend on the website, how often they visit, and the main purpose as to why they continue or have discontinued their use of the site.

Target Population

The target population that the study was interested in was K-12 students, college students, and people who are post studies. For the students who are from kindergarten to high school, assuming they will range from five to eighteen years of age.

Common characteristics among the target population include: motivation to study, interest in donating/helping others, and an interest in learning other subjects like languages or pre-collegiate level topics. In most cases not having a steady form of income or in some cases any income. The target population has a basic understanding of how to navigate a website and how to play a question and answer game.


Methods

The study methods were interviews and surveys. Interview questions included an ice breaker, an introduction question, key questions, a summary question, and a conclusion question. Some of the key questions were optional depending on the previous answer, this way we could ask specific questions if someone answered a certain way. The total amount of interviews needed done was ten and they would last up to 30 minutes. In addition, the interviews were constructed in an unstructured way so that more questions could be asked and obtain more information about the Freerice website.


The method for the online survey portion was very much up to the people who took the survey. A list of questions were created very similar to the interview questions, but more focused on understanding the users of Freerice. The survey questions were to be taken within 10 minutes, with some questions being optional. The method of directly finding these users of Freerice was through Facebook. Both the methods for this project were done with parental permission if a user was younger than 16 years old.
